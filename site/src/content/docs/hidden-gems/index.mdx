## Seed Gems

### Radiant Heat Is Not Heat

Steinmetz's opening lecture sharply separates radiation in transit from heat produced by absorption. This is a compact lesson in physical clarity.

### Alternating-Current Fields as Waves

Steinmetz describes AC circuit fields as wave phenomena whose wavelength is usually too large for ordinary propagation effects to be considered.

### Mathematics as Physical Translation

The symbolic method is not merely a convenience layer. In Steinmetz's AC writings, symbolic quantities preserve phase, opposition, and stored-energy exchange in a form the engineer can calculate.

### The Imaginary Unit Begins As Geometry

In the symbolic-method chapter, `j` is introduced through component geometry before it becomes the imaginary unit. That sequence is easy to miss in modern teaching, where the algebra often appears before the physical diagram.

### Reactance Is Not Resistance

Steinmetz's AC language keeps resistance and reactance distinct even though both are measured in ohms. This is a small mathematical fact with large conceptual consequences: one is power-consuming opposition, the other belongs to field storage and phase.

### Admittance Is Not A Shallow Reciprocal

Conductance and susceptance are not generally simple reciprocals of resistance and reactance. They are components of the reciprocal of complex impedance. This is a hidden lesson in why AC quantities must be treated structurally.

### Transients Expose What Steady State Hides

The transient sources are likely to become one of the richest parts of the archive because they show the temporary terms that disappear after steady operation but often decide insulation stress, line behavior, discharge behavior, and failure modes.

### Oscillation Requires Stored Energy Exchange

The transient source layer connects oscillation to inductance and capacity. This gives a direct bridge from circuit constants to energy exchange, damping, surge behavior, and high-frequency phenomena.

### Illumination Is Not Just Light Output

The illumination lectures distinguish radiation, visible effect, measurement, distribution, and human perception. That makes them useful for showing how Steinmetz moves between physical energy and practical engineering experience.
